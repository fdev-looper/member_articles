The Another Side of Navratri

The Tribal Perspective on Mahishasura Jayanti

While most of India celebrates Navratri, some communities observe Mahishasura Jayanti (Mahishasura Shahadat Diwas). For tribal groups like the Asur, Santhal, Kherwal, and Korku, Mahishasura is not a demon but their ancestor and king.

Historical Background

According to tribal tradition, Hudur Durga, a powerful ruler of Chaichampa, was betrayed by Aryans who sent a woman to marry him; she killed him on their wedding night. His people lost their lands, and this tragedy is mourned even today during Durga Puja through the Desai dance. Tribals believe mainstream history distorted their ancestor into a demon.

Mahishasura is remembered as a hero, king, and clan teacher (Kulguru). His death is mourned, not celebrated.

Legacy

The Name and Statue

The city Mysore derives from Mahishūru—"land of Mahisha". In Hindu mythology, he is a demon slain by Goddess Chamundeshwari, but tribal memory honors him as a respected ruler. The Mahishasura statue on Chamundi Hills, installed by the Wodeyar kings, symbolizes strength and power, reflecting the tribal perspective of him as a historic ruler.

Observance

  • Mourning and Remembrance: Reflecting historical loss.
  • Prayer and Veneration: Offerings and homage to honor him.
  • Cultural Events: Garlanding his idol during Hudur Durga or Hudur-Dussehra ceremonies.
  • Education & Awareness: Gatherings recall his legacy and historical injustice.
  • Food Practices: Avoiding onion, garlic, and meat as tamasic or rajasic foods.

Contrast with Mainstream Durga Puja

Durga Puja: Celebrates Mahishasura as a demon; symbolizes good triumphing over evil.

Mahishasura Jayanti: Celebrates him as a heroic king and ancestor; his death is mourned.

Modern Relevance

Tribal communities promote Mahishasura Jayanti to honor ancestry, preserve culture, and challenge mainstream narratives, highlighting injustices faced by their forefathers.

Objections and Controversies

1. Political & Religious

In 2017, HR Minister Smriti Irani called participants "anti-national" for distributing pamphlets perceived as derogatory toward Goddess Durga. In Mysuru, Mahisha Dasara celebrations faced counter-protests and police intervention.

2. Media & Public Perception

Media coverage sometimes frames the observance as challenging traditional narratives, and local authorities occasionally impose prohibitory orders to maintain public order.

Conclusion

Mahishasura Jayanti emphasizes the importance of historical memory and cultural identity. While contrasting with mainstream Durga Puja, it reminds us that history and mythology are interpreted differently by diverse communities, and these perspectives deserve respect and awareness.
